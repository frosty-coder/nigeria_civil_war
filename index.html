Overview

The Nigerian Civil War, also known as the Biafran War, lasted from July 6, 1967, to January 15, 1970. It was a conflict fought between the Nigerian government and the secessionist state of Biafra, which had declared its independence from Nigeria.

The war resulted in an estimated 1-3 million casualties, primarily due to starvation and disease. It is considered one of the most devastating humanitarian crises of the 20th century, highlighting the complex ethnic, political, and economic tensions that existed in post-colonial Nigeria.

Reasons for the War

Ethnic and Political Tensions

Nigeria gained independence from Britain in 1960, but ethnic divisions remained strong. The country was divided mainly among the Hausa-Fulani (North), Yoruba (West), and Igbo (East). After independence, these groups competed for political power, leading to tensions.

The 1966 Coups and Massacre of the Igbo

In January 1966, a group of mostly Igbo military officers led a coup, overthrowing the government. Many northern and western leaders were killed, but the coup failed to fully take over. A few months later, a counter-coup (July 1966) was staged by northern officers. This resulted in mass killings of Igbo people in the north, causing over 30,000 deaths and forcing many Igbo to flee back to their homeland.

Secession of Biafra

Due to the massacres and lack of security, Colonel Odumegwu Ojukwu, the governor of Eastern Nigeria, declared the Republic of Biafra on May 30, 1967. The Nigerian government, led by General Yakubu Gowon, saw this as a rebellion and decided to crush Biafra's secession.

Economic and Oil Interests

The eastern region of Nigeria (Biafra) had rich oil reserves, which were critical to the Nigerian economy. The Nigerian government and foreign powers (like Britain and the Soviet Union) wanted to keep Biafra under control to protect their oil interests.

Course of the War

May 30, 1967

Colonel Odumegwu Ojukwu declares the independent Republic of Biafra.

July 6, 1967

Nigerian forces invade Biafra, marking the official start of the war.

July - October 1967

Early Nigerian offensive. Biafran forces resist fiercely and launch a surprising counter-offensive into the Mid-Western region.

1968 - 1969

Nigeria imposes blockade, leading to humanitarian crisis. Images of starving Biafran children appear in global media, sparking international outrage.

Mid-1969

Final Nigerian offensive begins. Biafra has lost most of its territory.

January 10, 1970

Biafra's capital, Owerri, falls to Nigerian forces.

January 15, 1970

Biafra officially surrenders, ending the 30-month war.

Aftermath of the War

Casualties and Starvation

Estimated 1-3 million people died, mostly Biafran civilians from starvation. The war is considered one of the worst humanitarian disasters of the 20th century.

"No Victor, No Vanquished" Policy

General Yakubu Gowon declared there would be no punishment for Biafrans after the war. However, Igbo people faced economic discrimination, and their properties were seized in some regions.

Economic Discrimination

The government gave Igbos only £20 (about $50 at the time) from their bank accounts, no matter how much they had before the war.

Long-Term Effects

The war left deep ethnic divisions in Nigeria, which still affect the country today. Some Igbos still feel marginalized, and movements for Biafran independence continue.

International Involvement

Supporters of Nigeria

  • Britain (provided weapons and diplomatic support)
  • Soviet Union (supplied arms and aircraft)
  • Egypt (sent pilots and military support)
  • Most of the international community recognized Nigeria

Supporters of Biafra

  • France (supplied weapons secretly)
  • Israel (provided some assistance)
  • Humanitarian groups (like the Red Cross)
  • Only five countries officially recognized Biafra

Why Biafra Lost

  • Lack of international recognition
  • Economic blockade causing starvation
  • Superior Nigerian military strength
  • Internal Biafran struggles with leadership

Legacy of the War

Nigeria remains a deeply divided country, with ethnic and political tensions that continue to this day. The memory of the Biafran War still resonates, especially among the Igbo people.

Calls for Biafran independence continue, especially among young Igbos, with groups like the Indigenous People of Biafra (IPOB) advocating for secession.

The war shaped Nigeria's military structure and politics for decades to come. It also influenced humanitarian response protocols for future conflicts around the world.

The suffering of the Biafran people, especially children, became iconic images that changed how the world responds to humanitarian crises.
